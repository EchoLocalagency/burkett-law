The California Domestic Violence Prevention Act

California domestic violence restraining orders are issued under the Domestic Violence Prevention Act, or DVPA, at Family Code sections 6200 through 6460. The DVPA covers abuse between people in a defined relationship — spouses, former spouses, cohabitants, former cohabitants, dating partners, co-parents of a child, and close relatives. Abuse under the DVPA is broader than physical violence. It includes intentionally or recklessly causing bodily injury, sexual assault, placing another in reasonable apprehension of imminent serious bodily injury, and behavior that can be enjoined under Family Code section 6320 — harassment, stalking, threats, and disturbing the peace of the other party.

The 2020 amendments to section 6320 codified case-law expansion of "disturbing the peace" to include coercive control — a pattern of behavior that unreasonably interferes with a person's free will and personal liberty, including isolation, financial control, and reproductive coercion. That expansion matters because it lets DVROs address abusive patterns even when there is no physical incident.

How the process moves through court

Emergency Protective Order (EPO)

An EPO is a short-term order issued by a judge at the request of law enforcement, typically at the scene of an incident. It is good for up to seven days and is meant to bridge the gap until the petitioner can go to family court for a TRO. EPOs frequently include temporary custody and stay-away provisions.

Temporary Restraining Order (TRO)

A TRO is requested by filing Form DV-100 (Request for Domestic Violence Restraining Order) and a supporting declaration under penalty of perjury describing the abuse. In San Diego, the case is filed at the family-law division of the courthouse covering where the parties live. The court reviews the request the same day or next business day and, if the standard is met, issues a TRO good until the noticed hearing (typically within about 21 to 25 days). The respondent must be personally served with the TRO and hearing notice.

Noticed hearing on a permanent DVRO

At the noticed hearing, the court decides whether to issue a permanent DVRO. Both sides have the right to appear, testify, cross-examine witnesses, and present evidence. The petitioner must prove abuse by a preponderance of the evidence. If granted, a permanent DVRO can last up to five years and can be renewed. The terms often include no-contact, stay-away, move-out, temporary custody and visitation orders, temporary child and spousal support, possession of personal property, and firearm surrender.

DVRO cases move on a compressed calendar with permanent consequences. Whether you are the petitioner or the respondent, preparing the declaration, gathering exhibits, and lining up witnesses in the two to three weeks before the hearing is where the case is often decided.

Custody, firearms, and downstream effects

A DVRO reshapes several other legal areas at once. Family Code section 3044 creates a rebuttable presumption that awarding sole or joint custody to a parent found to have perpetrated domestic violence against the other parent or the child within the previous five years is not in the child's best interest. The presumption can be rebutted, but the record for rebuttal has to be built. Courts often order supervised visitation while the presumption is in play.

California and federal law prohibit a person subject to a DVRO from owning, possessing, or purchasing firearms while the order is in effect. The court orders relinquishment within 24 hours — by sale to a licensed dealer, transfer to law enforcement, or storage with an FFL — and requires proof of relinquishment filed with the court. Immigration consequences can also follow for non-citizens; a DVRO finding is not a conviction, but it can affect immigration proceedings and should be considered before the hearing.

After the DVRO is entered (or dropped), the custody, support, and property issues typically move over into a family-law case — either an existing dissolution or paternity, or a newly filed one — where they get fully adjudicated on the normal family-law track.

What I do for DVRO clients in San Diego

On the petitioner side, my work centers on the declaration and the hearing prep. The DV-100 declaration is often the most important document in the case — it has to describe specific incidents with dates, quotes, and specific behaviors, not general characterizations. I help clients organize the timeline, pull supporting evidence (photos, texts, medical records, police reports), and prepare for cross-examination at the hearing.

On the respondent side, my work centers on a full defense of the record. That means reviewing the DV-100 line by line, preparing a responsive declaration on Form DV-120, and organizing evidence that responds to the specific allegations rather than making general denials. A permanent DVRO with a section 3044 finding reshapes custody for years and triggers firearm surrender, so the defense has to be built with those downstream stakes in mind.

Frequently asked questions about DVROs in San Diego

What is a domestic violence restraining order in California?

A domestic violence restraining order (DVRO) is a civil order issued under the California Domestic Violence Prevention Act (Family Code sections 6200 through 6460) that protects one household member from abuse by another. It can include no-contact and stay-away provisions, move-out orders, temporary custody and visitation, temporary support, and firearm surrender.

What is the difference between an EPO, a TRO, and a permanent DVRO?

An Emergency Protective Order (EPO) is a short-term order issued at the request of law enforcement, usually good for up to seven days. A Temporary Restraining Order (TRO) is issued by the family court on an ex parte basis and typically lasts until the noticed hearing. A permanent DVRO is issued after a full contested hearing and can last up to five years, with a possibility of renewal, and in some cases can be permanent.

How does a DVRO affect child custody?

Family Code section 3044 creates a rebuttable presumption that awarding sole or joint custody to a parent who has been found to have perpetrated domestic violence against the other parent or the child within the previous five years is not in the child's best interest. That presumption can be rebutted, but it substantially affects the custody analysis, and courts often order supervised visitation while the presumption is in play.

Do I need proof to get a DVRO?

At the initial TRO stage, the standard is a written declaration under penalty of perjury describing the abuse. At the noticed hearing on a permanent DVRO, the petitioner must prove abuse by a preponderance of the evidence. Evidence typically includes witness testimony, text messages, photographs, medical records, and police reports. The respondent has the right to appear, testify, cross-examine witnesses, and present a defense.

Can a DVRO order the respondent to give up firearms?

Yes. California and federal law prohibit a person subject to a DVRO from owning, possessing, or purchasing firearms while the order is in effect. On issuance, the court orders the respondent to relinquish any firearms within 24 hours (or immediately, if in court) by sale to a licensed dealer or transfer to law enforcement, and file proof of relinquishment with the court.
